About Me

I'm an aspiring data scientist and a Ph.D. student in mathematics at The University of Texas at Austin. I enjoy applying my quantitative skills to extract wisdom from data through descriptive visualizations, statistical analysis, and machine learning. My academic research focuses on understanding elusive topological objects called 4-dimensional manifolds. These two interests are both grounded in an appreciation for creative ways to approach high-dimensional problems.


I'm also passionate about contributing to the field of social services. Most recently, I spent eight months as a case manager at The Wright House helping victims of HIV living below the poverty line.

My Projects

  • Who said it? juliaben.net/t/who-said-it obama-or-bush

    In this project, I explored speeches given by Obama and Bush through webscraping, natural language processing, and machine learning in Python. Using a dataset of scraped transcripts, I trained a support vector classifier to identify whether a radio address was given by Obama or Bush with greater than 99% accuracy. Along the way, I experimented with Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning. Additionally, I developed a game (available through the link above) that allows users to compete against this model and investigate how it makes decisions through interactive plots. The model was built with scikit-learn and MOE using tools from NLTK, and the frontend was created with Flask and D3.js.

  • Coffee & Demographics. juliaben.net/t/coffee coffee-and-demographics

    I developed a Shiny application (available through the link above) that describes one approach to working with geospatial data and the U.S. Census. Guided by interactive maps, this carefully details the creation of a dataset that addresses how the spread and density of coffee shops in major U.S. cities is related to their demographic characteristics. All processing was completed in R, using sp and three related packages for managing spatial objects.
